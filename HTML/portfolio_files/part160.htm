16.2 THE DATA AND SOFTWARE

The choice of data for the backtest is largely determined by the factors that the portfolio manager uses in his or her model of stock returns.3 For our backtest, we collected five major categories of data: fundamental data, prices and returns, analyst forecasts, social-issue data, and macroeconomic indicators. The fundamental data are from Standard & Poor’s Compustat-Industrial database, which contains items from the balance sheets, income statements, and cash-flow statements of the companies. Price and return data are from the University of Chicago’s Center for Research in Security Prices (CRSP) database and the Compustat Price, Dividends, and Earnings (PDE) database. The PDE database contains basic information rela- ted to stock pricing, such as month-end closing prices, shares out- standing, monthly dividends per share, and earnings per share, whereas the CRSP database includes daily prices and trading vol- umes. Analyst forecasts are from the Institutional Broker Estimates System (IBES) database, and the social-issue data are from KLD Research and Analytics, Inc. Macroeconomic data together with index future data are from Bloomberg.

We collected monthly data whenever possible, although most of the fundamental and social-issue data are annual.4 The funda- mental data are stored in the month in which the company’s fiscal year ended. All social-issue data are stored in December. Certain macroeconomic data are quarterly, and they are recorded in the quarter-end months. For certain technical factors, monthly values have been created from daily price and volume data.

The investment universe was selected from the Compustat- Industrial and Compustat PDE databases. First, we ranked all com- panies in the Compustat database by market capitalization. For the end of each month of data from January 1976 through December 2003, we ranked all domestic U.S. stocks and stored the top 3000. We also ranked foreign stocks [(such as American Depository Receipts (ADRs)] and stored the top 300. In addition, we stored all stocks included in the Standard & Poor’s (S&P) 500, S&P 600, and S&P 400 each month if they were not stored already.5 For the pur- pose of computation, we stored the entire data history of each stock that entered the investment universe in any month.

Our next step was to merge data from five separate databases. Stocks can be identified by CUSIP number or exchange ticker, but these identifiers change over time, and worse yet, are often recycled. For this reason, financial databases use their own, permanent, alpha- numeric identifiers for stocks. Linking the permanent identifiers, though, can be a bit of a challenge.
