Tables of Futures Leverage Multipliers Needed to Achieve Various Degrees of Leverage


In this appendix we compiled a series of tables with various base- line s values of a portfolio manager and the futures leverage multi- plier {[ * + ( — 1)  s]/f q} which when multiplied by the ratio of the portfolio’s value to the index’s value will determine the num- ber of appropriate futures contracts to purchase to alter the  of the total portfolio to the desired level. For these tables, we used S&P 500 and NASDAQ 100 futures because they are the most liquid. In both cases we assumed that the benchmark for the portfolio man-

ager was the S&P 500; hence all  s are with respect to the S&P 500 Index. However, the leverage ratio, l, is always with respect to the actual portfolio. As we stated earlier, our measure of leverage is not the conventional measure of leverage. However, they are the same when the futures positions are taken to enhance the exposure of the portfolio. Thus, in the tables, anytime the futures leverage multiplier is positive, the leverage ratio, l, will coincide with the conventional measure of leverage. However, when the futures leverage multiplier is negative (indicating to sell futures), the lever- age ratio, l, will not coincide with the conventional measure of leverage. To obtain the conventional measure of leverage when the futures multiplier is negative, use the formula l c = (1—l)+1, where l c is the symbol for the conventional leverage measure. Our lever- age ratio can also be interpreted as the net dollar exposure of the entire position. We also assume that the portfolio manager does not hold cash ( = 0).

Thus suppose that a portfolio manager is managing a portfo- lio with a s = 1.6 with respect to the benchmark, which we have assumed is the S&P 500, and he or she would like to increase the overall  of the portfolio to 3. His or her portfolio has a value of

In Table 12C.1, we find the 1.6 value under the s column and then slide over until we reach the column under * = 3, which tells the portfolio manager that the futures leverage multiplier is 0.0056. The ratio of the portfolio’s value to the index’s value is 76,923.08 ($100 M/1300). Thus, the appropriate number of futures contracts

to purchase to achieve the desired position is 430.76 (=0.0056 · 76,923.08). Thus, in practice, we would round to the nearest integer and actually purchase 431 futures contracts. This will achieve his or her desired * and a leverage ratio of 2.4 (the number below the futures multiplier in the table). To accomplish the same sort of * with respect to the benchmark using NASDAQ 100 futures would require purchasing 422.73 (=0.0093 · 45,454.55) futures contracts but result in a lowered leveraged position of 1.93 owing to the high

 of the NASDAQ 100 versus the benchmark (for this example we assumed the NASDAQ index was at 2200). In practice, we would round to the nearest integer and purchase 423 NASDAQ 100 futures contracts.

In the two preceding examples, our leverage ratio, l, is equal to the conventional measure of leverage. Suppose instead, we wish to use S&P 500 futures to reduce our overall  to 1. In this particu- lar case we find the value of 1.6 under the s column and slide over until we reach the column under  = 1 which tells the portfolio

manager that the futures leverage multiplier is — 0.0024. The appropriate number of futures to short will be 184.62(= — 0.0024 . 76,923.08). This will achieve the desired  and a leverage ratio or net dollar exposure of 0.40. The conventional leverage measure in this example would be 1.6 [(1 — 0.4) + 1]. If we were instead to use NASDAQ 100 futures to achieve the same position, we would sell

181.81 (= — 0.0040 . 45,454.55) futures contracts resulting in a lever- age ration or net dollar exposure of 0.60. The conventional leverage measure in this example would be 1.4[(1 — 0.6) + 1].
