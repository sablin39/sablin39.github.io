Consider the case of the index portfolio manager who wishes to short the entire index. We already showed how to find, for the long portfolio, the number of contracts to buy to reach a desired overall portfolio . With shorting, the only difference is that rather than buying the futures, the manager will short them. The equations for long-only portfolios apply to the short-only portfolios, the only difference being that the number of contracts will be negative (reflecting the fact that the  * is negative).

Given an S&P 500 Index trading at 1000, a multiplier for the large S&P 500 contracts of 250, a sum of cash equal to $100 million, and a  * = —2, the appropriate number of futures contracts to sell would be 800. (Remember that f = 1 when the benchmark is the S&P 500). The portfolio manager should sell the 800 futures con- tracts at a cost of (qNf Ft)mf in margin balance. The remainder is held in cash, earning the interest rate.

As for rebalancing the position, the same concepts apply that

apply to long-only portfolios. The triggers for rebalancing simply change. A market rally, for instance, would be cause to short futures contracts. A market decline would call for the purchase of futures contracts.

As for liquidity buffering, the portfolio manager also should follow the guidelines of liquidity buffering for long-only portfolios, except that now he or she should buy out-of-the-money call options rather than out-of-the-money put options.
