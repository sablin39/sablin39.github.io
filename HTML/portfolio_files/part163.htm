16.5 THE FACTORS

We discussed factor choice at length in Chapter 4. In this section we list factors that we think might be important in explaining stock returns and in generating  B. We also show statistics on the signi- ficance of various factors.

Before testing factors, the data need to be organized and cleaned. All researchers know the immense challenges of data organization and cleaning.20 There are really three steps in prepar- ing the data for use in testing. The first is to check the general con- sistency and accuracy of the data. Are all the financial variables for each stock computed correctly, or are there some clear data errors? Errors could include ratios that are glaringly off (e.g., a P/E ratio of 180,000) or, harder to detect, ratios computed with erroneous inputs. The second step is to check that the data are recorded as of the correct dates. Some data vendors record earnings per share (EPS) as of the quarter in which they were earned even though they were not reported until a few months after the quarter ended. This creates look-ahead bias. Any data recorded this way should be

The third step in preparing the data is the arduous task of data organization, which includes anything else necessary to get the data in the right shape to be read into the model- and portfolio- building software. One problem encountered at this point is survivorship bias. Owing to mergers, acquisitions, delistings, bank- ruptcies, and other events, some stocks that existed in the past do not exist today. If a backtest uses only data on stocks that exist today, the results of the test will be biased, usually upward. A back- test always should include all the data available on any stock that meets the investment universe criteria on any given date. Some databases, such as CRSP, provide returns of extinct stocks based on their likely selling prices at the time they stopped trading, as esti- mated by their last trading prices or the prices at which investors actually would have been able to sell shares back to the company or to creditors but not through the stock exchange. If the portfolio manager does not have access to a database with these sorts of estimated returns, he or she will have to make up his or her own sensible rules for dealing with extinct stocks in the backtest.

To begin the factor testing for our backtest, we considered

some of the most important factors that might be related to stock returns. Our initial selection of factors, drawn from the factors described in Chapter 4, was based on theoretical reasoning. In Tables 16.9 and 16.10 we name the factors we chose, the reason for choosing them, and our ex-ante belief about how they relate to the cross section of stock returns.

In order to test the factors, we first created programs that com- puted the historical factors for every stock in our database. We ran these programs at monthly intervals to create a historical series of monthly factor exposures for each stock, all the while being vigi- lant about look-ahead bias. The historical P/B ratios, for example, we computed by multiplying the price of a stock by the number of shares outstanding and then dividing that product by the total common equity. For common equity, we allowed for a lag in report- ing of three months, so we searched for the book value as reported three months prior to the month for which we created the P/B ratio. For a detailed description of all our factor formulas, see Tables 16A.1, 16A.2, and 16A.3 in Appendix 16A.

This avoids look-ahead bias by recognizing the one-month delay in CPI releases. This type of logic was applied to all our macroeconomic variables so that we created a time series of macroeconomic factors that would have been available at the end of the month. We then ran individual regressions of each stock’s monthly returns against each monthly economic factor over a period of no less than two years and no more than four years. If there were fewer than two years of return data for a stock, we did not estimate its macroeconomic exposure, but if there were between two and four years of data, we used all the data to estimate the factor exposure. To keep the esti- mates fresh, we constructed the factor exposures on a rolling basis for each month looking back a maximum of four years.

The resulting factor exposures give us a direct relationship

between factor values and stock returns. For example, the factor exposure of Anheuser-Busch (Budweiser) to GDP growth each year is mostly negative. The estimate for December 1979 is —0.00044. If GDP growth for the quarter prior to December 1979 were 5%, then the expected return on the stock would be —0.0022%. Similarly, if GDP growth for the prior quarter were —5%, we would expect the stock price to have gone up by 0.0022%. The small negative exposure fits with the intuition that BUD is a mildly defensive stock. There were some outliers in the factor exposure estimates as well. Some practitioners will carefully remove these outliers, instead we chose not to remove the extreme observations in our backtests.21 One out- lier that sticks out in the data is AWGI, a funeral home and services company. The stock has a strongly negative exposure to GDP growth mainly because it experienced an extreme decline during 2002 and 2003 (not necessarily because fewer people die in a good economy). During this period, AWGI’s price plummeted, whereas GDP growth was moderate.

The acute reader will notice that many of our factors have few observations or no listed results. The reason for this is that the analyst-related and social factors were not available until the 1990s. Some of our industry-related factors are also lacking values because the Global Industry Classification System (GICS) codes that we use did not begin until 1994. Since some of the factors with missing val- ues are of particular interest to quantitative portfolio managers, we included factor tests for later periods in Appendix 16B on the accom- panying CD. We cannot use those tests for forming our portfolios, however, because they are in the out-of-sample period.

With the results in Tables 16.11 and 16.12, we proceeded to select the factors for our stock-return models. Our goal was to build a Z-score model, a fundamental factor model, and an economic factor model. Our selection proceeded as follows.

First, we set a minimum t-statistic of 1.65 for the  coefficient or for the zero-investment average return. A t-statistic of 1.65 cor- responds to a 90% confidence interval when the number of obser- vations is large. We eliminated any factors that did not exceed this minimum. To select factors for the fundamental factor model, we used the t-statistics of the  coefficients as our criterion, whereas for the economic factor model, we used the t-statistics of the zero- investment portfolio average returns as our criterion. For our fun- damental factor model, the tests left us with 12 potential factors out of 64 nonmacroeconomic factors. Remember, the fundamental fac- tor model cannot use economic variables, so we did not consider them for it. For the economic factor model, the tests left us with 23 potential factors out of 83 factors.22

Then we asked whether the sign of the coefficients was in accor-

dance with what we expected from theory. If a coefficient did not correspond with our ex-ante theoretical beliefs, we eliminated the factor. For the fundamental factor model, there were four variables that did not coincide with our ex-ante thoughts.
