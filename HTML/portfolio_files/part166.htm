16.8 VARIATIONS ON THE BASELINE PORTFOLIO

Our baseline portfolio for each of the three models is the tracking portfolio with four constraints: a constraint on the expected excess return, a no-short-selling constraint, a full-investment constraint, and a trading-volume constraint. For purposes of illustration, we imposed additional constraints on the economic factor model’s baseline portfolio to create six variations on that portfolio.

For the first variation, we added a sector-weight constraint that required the sector weights of the portfolio to be identical to those of the benchmark. We used the Global Industrial Classification System (GICS) to identify 10 major sectors of stocks and then set up our portfolio so that its weights in those 10 sectors matched the benchmark’s weights in those sectors.

16.8.1 Transactions Costs

Portfolio managers often backtest strategies without considering transactions costs, an ommission that colors the test results an unrealistically rosy hue. The portfolio strategy that yields the best results in a test without transactions costs may involve high turnover that will chip away at the manager’s B when put into practice. Ignoring transactions costs also can bias the manager toward selecting smaller-cap stocks despite the fact that small- caps’ trading costs actually are higher than the costs associated with large-cap stocks in similar lines of business. A fair backtest must deal with transactions costs in some way.

Our backtest period is 1995–2003. From conversations with traders, we found that the general wisdom is that commissions on trades in 1995 were around 5 to 7 cents per share, whereas by 2003 they were probably around 2 cents per share. These are rule-of- thumb numbers that a manager could use for backtesting, but we decided to do a little more research on the subject.

The Plexus Group keeps track of institutional trading costs for

U.S. equities.29 The Plexus Group divides trading costs into several categories, including commissions, market impact, delay, and opportu- nity cost. Commissions are the explicit fee paid for brokerage services to enact a trade. Market impact is any change in the mar- ket price of a stock owing to supply/demand imbalances caused by placing a trade. Impact is measured as the difference between

the price at which the broker receives the order and the execution price. Delay summarizes the interday costs of price movements that occur while orders are held over from one day to the next owing to either lack of liquidity or failure to act. Opportunity cost equals the percentage of unexecuted trades multiplied by the 30- day return of the unexecuted trades. Opportunity cost results from delaying execution to lessen market impact, from not being able to make the execution at all, or from abandoning part of the trade because the market has turned against the strategy. If an invest- ment manager’s strategy envisioned the purchase of 100,000 shares, and only 50,000 were actually purchased, the lost potential returns on the remainder represent an opportunity cost. The Plexus Group uses the average cost per trade for institutional money managers to measure opportunity cost.

These transactions costs can really add up. For example,

according to Plexus, in the first quarter of 2005, commissions contributed 11 basis points, market impact contributed 13 basis points, delay contributed 29 basis points, and opportunity costs contributed 6 basis points to the total trading costs. In total, 60 basis points per trade were just transactions costs. Your model’s B had better be pretty high to counteract that! For trades of 25,000 shares or more, Plexus advises that managers define the average transactions costs as commissions plus market impact plus delay, whereas for transactions of 10,000 shares or less, it is suffi- cient to use commissions plus market impact. 16.8.2 Taxes

Backtesting without considering tax consequences also can seri- ously distort the test results. As we emphasized in Chapter 11, what matters to investors in the end is the after-tax return, and the after-tax return can be quite different from the before-tax return. Taxes create two tasks for portfolio managers. The first is to calcu- late and report both the before-tax and after-tax returns of the port- folio. The second is to consider actively tax-managed portfolios that generate extra returns through tax savings.

Calculating the after-tax return is simple in principle but can be demanding in practice. Tax rates vary for different investors, so it is not always obvious which tax rates to apply to long-term capi- tal gains, short-term capital gains, and dividends. Keeping track of tax lots requires additional effort. For the purpose of our backtest,

we assumed a 15% rate on long-term capital gains. Since most of our hypothetical trades occurred annually, we rarely incurred short- term capital gains or losses, and we did not treat them separately. Tax rates on dividend income vary significantly among different types of investors, so we ignored dividend taxes in the backtest. We did keep track of tax lots and applied the tax-lot selection method detailed in Chapter 11 to minimize the tax burden. In this method, the potential tax burden of each tax lot is calculated before trading so that the first trade uses the lot with the lowest potential taxes.

For the actively tax-managed portfolio, we adopted the loss- harvesting approach. Loss-harvesting, which involves selling shares that are trading at less than the purchase price, generates capital losses and reduces the overall tax burden of the portfolio. Loss harvesting every single losing share maximizes the tax benefit but has the undesirable effect of increasing the portfolio’s tracking error with respect to the benchmark. For our backtesting example, we implemented a more conservative form of loss harvesting. We created the tax-managed portfolio in two steps. In the first step, we decided which stocks to buy and which stocks to sell, without con- sidering taxes. In the second step, we decided how many shares to buy and how many shares to sell using the loss-harvesting tech- nique. This two-step procedure ensured that the loss harvesting did not raise the portfolio’s tracking error too much.

16.8.4 Market Neutral

To test the market-neutral strategy, we created a dollar-neutral portfolio that was -neutral to each of the factors in the economic factor model. Since we already had found the factor-exposure- matched tracking portfolio, we simply took a long position in this optimal tracking portfolio and a short position in the benchmark. The resulting portfolio was dollar-neutral because the long posi- tion and the short position were the same in dollar amount. The resulting portfolio also was -neutral because the tracking portfolio and the benchmark portfolio had the same factor exposures.

In addition to this, we also created a dollar-neutral, sector- neutral portfolio from the sector-matched tracking portfolio and the benchmark. We took a long position in the optimal tracking portfo- lio and a short position in the benchmark. The resulting portfolio was both sector- and dollar-neutral.
