16.7 PARAMETER STABILITY AND THE REBALANCING FREQUENCY

Before building the stock-return models, it is useful to test for para- meter stability. Tenet 6 of QEPM states that quantitative models should reflect persistent and stable patterns. Testing for parameter stability determines how frequently a stock-return model must be reestimated to ensure that it describes persistent, stable patterns in the data. Recall that in the factor-selection stage we regressed the return on the factor exposure for each month. By pooling the monthly regressions, we were able to test whether the factor pre- mium was stable over time. For instance, consider the regression of the return on the total asset turnover ratio:

ri,t+1 = t+1 + ft+1 TATi,t + Ei,t+1 (16.2)

In this equation, TATi,t is the total asset turnover of firm i in month t, and ft is the factor premium of month t+1. This equation was estimated for each month t from January 1980 through December 1994. At the end of this procedure, we had 180 sets of estimates for  and f, each set corresponding to one month.

By pooling 180 regressions and running them at the same time, we tested whether f is stable over time. Note that in the pooled regression, we did not impose any restriction on f or ; we still estimated 180 f ’s and 180  ’s. The resulting estimates are iden- tical to those we obtain from the month-by-month regressions. The pooled regression makes hypothesis testing easier without adding any restrictions.

March 1980 were identical. The null hypothesis was that all three f ’s were identical; the alternative hypothesis was that they were not identical. If the p-value of this sort of test is high, we have more confidence in the null hypothesis. If the p-value is low, the alterna- tive hypothesis is more likely to be true. We used the conventional cutoff value of 5%. With a cutoff value of 5%, if the p-value is high- er than 5%, the null hypothesis is accepted. If the p-value is lower than 5%, the null hypothesis is rejected.

We ran this same test for the next quarter (i.e., for the f of April 1980, the f of May 1980, and the f of June 1980), and we repeated it until we had completed all 60 quarters of data from 1980 through 1994.26 Of the 60 tests performed, we rejected the null hypothesis 31 times, a rejection rate of about 0.52 (= 31/60). This rejection rate indicates that the premium on total asset turnover is more or less stable in a quarter.

The same testing procedure is easily extended to any other lengths of time. We carried out the analysis for periods of six months, one year, and two years, in addition to the quarterly periods. Since we did not use overlapping data in the test, we performed the test on 30 six-month periods (Jan 1980–Jun 1980, Jul 1980–Dec 1980, …, Jul 1994–Dec 1994); on 15 one-year periods (Jan 1980–Dec 1980, Jan 1981–Dec 1981, …, Jan 1994–Dec 1994); and on 7 two-year periods (Jan 1980–Dec 1981, …, Jan 1992–Dec 1993).

While the evidence for parameter stability is mixed, we could not make the period between reestimations too short. Certain minimum amounts of time must elapse between reestimations in order to estimate the risk—particularly the nonsystematic risk—of individual stocks.
